Terminating vs Repeating ‚Äî Intuition

Big idea: A fraction \( \tfrac{a}{b} \) has a terminating decimal when we can make a denominator like \(10, 100, 1000,\dots\).

That happens exactly when \(b\) is made only by multiplying 2s and 5s (e.g., \(4=2\cdot2\), \(20=2\cdot2\cdot5\)). If we need any other number (like 3 or 7), the decimal will repeat.

Quick test: Reduce \( \tfrac{a}{b} \). Keep dividing the denominator by 2 while you can, then by 5 while you can. If you end at 1 ‚Üí terminating; otherwise ‚Üí repeating.

Tester (enter a denominator \(b\) from a reduced fraction):
Examples:
  • \( \tfrac14 = 0.25\) (since \(4=2\cdot2\)) ‚Üí terminating
  • \( \tfrac25 = 0.4\) (since \(5\)) ‚Üí terminating
  • \( \tfrac16 = 0.1666\dots\) (needs a 3) ‚Üí repeating

Repeating Decimals ‚Üí Fractions (with \( \overline{\phantom{9}} \))

We use an overline to show the repeating part: \(0.\overline{3}\), \(0.\overline{27}\), \(3.\overline{6}\).

Rule A ‚Äî pure repeat: If \(x=0.\overline{R}\) and the block \(R\) has \(r\) digits, then \[ x=\frac{R}{\underbrace{99\ldots 9}_{r\ \text{nines}}}. \] Examples: \(0.\overline{3}=\tfrac{3}{9}=\tfrac13\), \(0.\overline{27}=\tfrac{27}{99}=\tfrac{3}{11}\).

Rule B ‚Äî whole number + repeat: If \(x=I.\overline{R}\) (same \(R\) of length \(r\)), then \[ x=\frac{I\cdot(\underbrace{99\ldots9}_{r})+R}{\underbrace{99\ldots9}_{r}}. \] Example: \(3.\overline{6}=\dfrac{3\cdot 9+6}{9}=\dfrac{33}{9}=\dfrac{11}{3}\).
